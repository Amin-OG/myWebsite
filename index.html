2D Two-Phase Oil Reservoir Simulation

Oil reservoir simulation of two-phase flow with a water source (aquifer) models the behavior of oil and water in a reservoir. It predicts oil extraction over time by analyzing how the aquifer and water injection wells maintain pressure and support oil production from oil wells.

The simulation accounts for fluid interactions, pressure changes, and production rates, enabling engineers to optimize extraction strategies and assess reservoir performance. Overall, it is essential for sustainable and efficient management of oil resources.

This MATLAB-based reservoir simulator uses the Black Oil model with the IMPES method to analyze two-phase flow (water and oil) in a 2D single-porosity environment.

It simulates various production mechanisms and allows for unlimited wells with customizable conditions. The program calculates pressure and saturation using iterative methods and performs automatic material balance checks.

Streamline Simulation

Streamline simulation of water flooding is a technique used in reservoir engineering to visualize and predict the movement of fluids, like water, through an oil reservoir. By creating a digital model of the reservoir, including well locations and geological properties, the simulation calculates the flow paths of fluids using streamlines. These streamlines show how injected water moves through the reservoir to push oil towards production wells. This method is efficient, requires less computational power, and provides clear visualizations, enabling engineers to optimize water injection strategies and improve oil recovery.
In this problem, the process of injecting water into a reservoir containing three production wells at the corners is simulated.

Heat Transfer Simulation


Heat transfer in a plate primarily occurs through conduction, where thermal energy moves from a hotter region to a cooler region due to molecular collisions. When one side of the plate is heated, the molecules in that area gain energy and transfer it to neighboring molecules, creating a flow of heat through the material. The rate of heat transfer depends on the material's thermal conductivity, the thickness of the plate, the surface area, and the temperature difference between the two sides. This process can be described by Fourier's Law, which quantifies the heat transfer rate based on these factors.

Integrating scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq Data for Regulatory Interaction Inference

This project provides a comprehensive guide on integrating scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq data to infer regulatory interactions using the GLUE framework. It starts with data preparation, where feature embeddings from both modalities are processed to extract highly-variable features and create a skeleton graph. This graph is essential for limiting the search space for regulatory connections and identifying potential regulatory interactions between RNA genes and ATAC peaks through cosine similarity scores. The model training phase then focuses on developing a predictive model using these embeddings to estimate regulatory scores, with a strong emphasis on parameter tuning and cross-validation to ensure accurate predictions. The model's performance is rigorously evaluated using various metrics. Following this, regulatory inference utilizes the trained model and embeddings to determine significant regulatory connections, applying a Q-value threshold of 0.05 for significance. Visualization of these regulatory interactions is achieved using pyGenomeTracks, which involves preparing BED and links files and configuring track files for effective representation. The project concludes with the construction of the TF-gene regulatory network, detailing the generation of a coexpression-based draft network with GRNBoost2, refinement using TF motif/ChIP-seq data, and pruning with cis-regulatory data to produce a refined network.

Single cell RNA Seq Data Analysis

Dimension reduction techniques like PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP are crucial for analyzing single-cell RNA-seq data by simplifying the high-dimensional gene expression data into fewer dimensions, making it easier to visualize and interpret biological patterns and cell heterogeneity. The process involves several steps: loading and preprocessing the data, filtering cells and genes based on quality metrics, normalizing and log-transforming the data, and identifying highly variable genes. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) reduces dimensionality by focusing on principal components, followed by t-SNE and UMAP for further visualization. Clustering using algorithms like Leiden, along with differential expression analysis through methods such as t-test, Wilcoxon rank-sum test, and logistic regression, helps identify key marker genes and cell types, which are then visualized and compared to reveal distinct clusters and their biological significance.

Real-time EEG Learning

BioExam is an adaptive online learning system that integrates a Human-in-the-Loop Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithm with real-time EEG biofeedback to address the challenges posed by the COVID-19-induced shift to online education. Unlike existing tools like SuperMemo and Anki, which rely on heuristic methods, BioExam uses RL to balance learning new material and reviewing old topics while considering students' physical and mental states.

This system incorporates EEG signals as secondary rewards to enhance learning efficiency and reduce trial numbers. By initializing with a pre-trained network based on human memory models, BioExam simulates student behavior to optimize performance. Utilizing low-cost EEG headsets and smartwatches, the system monitors attentiveness and physical conditions, providing real-time feedback to students to improve their learning outcomes.

Muscle Sympathetic Nerve Activity Clustering

Muscle Sympathetic Nerve Activity (MSNA) signal clustering using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) involves reducing the complexity of high-dimensional signal data to uncover meaningful patterns. First, the raw MSNA signals are preprocessed to remove noise and normalize the data. PCA is then applied to transform the data into a lower-dimensional space by identifying the principal components that capture the most significant variance. This reduced feature set is used for clustering, typically with algorithms like k-means, to group similar signal segments. Each cluster represents distinct patterns in the MSNA signals, which can be analyzed for physiological or clinical insights.
